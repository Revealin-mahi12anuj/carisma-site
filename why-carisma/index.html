Peer-Reviewed Study
01
Discovery of a low-glycaemic index potato and relationship with starch digestion in vitro
Ek, Wang, Copeland & Brand-Miller · British Journal of Nutrition (2014) · University of Sydney

This is the origin study — the research that first identified Carisma as a genuinely low-GI potato. Researchers at the University of Sydney, working with Professor Jennie Brand-Miller (co-creator of the glycaemic index concept itself), tested seven common and newly introduced potato cultivars under International Standard Organisation (ISO) testing guidelines.

The cultivars produced a wide spread of glycaemic index values, from 53 all the way to 103 — an enormous range for what most consumers think of as a single food category. Of the seven, only Carisma was classified as low-GI. Nicola came in as medium-GI, and the remaining five cultivars were all classified high-GI.

The study also examined why: in-vitro starch hydrolysis and amylose content analysis were used to connect each cultivar's digestibility to its measured glycaemic response, giving the finding a mechanistic basis rather than a one-off observation.

Key finding: Of 7 potato cultivars tested under ISO guidelines, Carisma was the only one classified as Low GI.

Academic Review
02
Starchy Carbohydrates in a Healthy Diet: The Role of the Humble Potato
Robertson et al. · Nutrients (MDPI), 2018

This is a broader academic review examining the potato's place in a healthy diet — moving past the outdated idea that all potatoes are nutritionally equal. The review draws together evidence on glycaemic response, resistant starch, and how cultivar and cooking method change a potato's health profile.

Carisma is cited directly as a demonstrated example of a naturally low-GI potato cultivar, referencing the University of Sydney findings. The review explains the underlying mechanism: Carisma's starch has a notably higher gelatinisation onset temperature than other cultivars, meaning more of its starch resists rapid digestion and converts to sugar more slowly — the biological basis for a lower glycaemic response.

Key finding: Carisma's higher starch gelatinisation temperature is the mechanistic reason its GI stays low even after cooking.
Read the review on MDPI
Health Platform
03
Can Diabetics Eat Potatoes?
Apollo247 (Apollo Hospitals) — Consumer Health Blog

Apollo247, one of India's largest health platforms, addresses a question that keeps many diabetics and pre-diabetics away from potatoes altogether. The article explains that not all potatoes carry the same risk — variety matters as much as portion size.

Carisma potatoes are named directly as one of the better choices for people managing diabetes, specifically because of their lower starch content compared to high-starch varieties like Russet. This is independent Indian medical-platform validation of the same underlying science — written for consumers, not researchers.

Key finding: Carisma named directly, by an Indian health platform, as a better potato choice for diabetics due to lower starch.

Trade Press
04
Nutrispud Line of Potatoes Answers Consumer Demand for Healthier Options
FreshPlaza — North America Produce Trade Press

FreshPlaza covers the global produce and horticulture trade. This piece reports on EarthFresh's "Nutrispud" line, built around the Carisma cultivar and marketed as a Low Glycemic Response potato — described as the first potato product on the market to offer naturally slow-release carbohydrates.

The article positions this as a direct response to consumer demand for healthier staple carbohydrates, and frames Carisma's low-GI trait as a genuine point of commercial and agricultural differentiation — not just a health claim, but a supply-chain and varietal story that the produce trade itself considers newsworthy.

Key finding: Independent trade press coverage confirms Carisma-based products are marketed internationally as the first naturally slow-release-carb potato.
